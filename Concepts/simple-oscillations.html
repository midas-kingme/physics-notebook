01. The Principle

Hooke's Law & Dynamics

At the heart of all oscillation is a Restoring Force. For an ideal spring, this force is linearly proportional to the displacement from equilibrium ($x=0$) but acts in the opposite direction.

$$F_{restoring} = -kx$$

Combining this with Newton's Second Law ($F=ma$), we derive the governing differential equation:

$$m\frac{d^2x}{dt^2} = -kx \quad \Rightarrow \quad \frac{d^2x}{dt^2} + \frac{k}{m}x = 0$$

The solution to this second-order differential equation is a sinusoidal function:

$$x(t) = A \cos(\omega t + \phi)$$
Real World Analogy

The "Invisible Leash"

Imagine a dog (the mass) tied to a post (equilibrium) by a rubber bungee cord (the spring). The further the dog runs away, the harder the cord pulls back.

02. Conservation

The Dance of Energy

In an ideal system, energy is never lost; it merely transforms between Kinetic ($K$) and Potential ($U$).

$$K = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 \quad \quad U = \frac{1}{2}kx^2$$

The total mechanical energy $E$ remains constant throughout the cycle.

$$E_{total} = \frac{1}{2}kA^2 = constant$$
Real World Analogy

The Playground Swing

Think of a child on a swing. At the very top of the arc (highest point), the child stops for a split second (maximum Potential Energy, zero Kinetic Energy). At the very bottom, they are moving fastest (maximum Kinetic Energy, minimum Potential Energy). The total energy of the swing persists.

03. Reality

Damped Oscillation

Real systems lose energy to friction. We model this as a damping force proportional to velocity: $F_d = -bv$.

$$m\frac{d^2x}{dt^2} + b\frac{dx}{dt} + kx = 0$$
Real World Analogy

Car Shock Absorbers

Without shocks, your car would bounce up and down endlessly after every bump (simple harmonic motion). Shock absorbers use viscous fluid to dissipate this energy, quickly damping out the oscillation so the ride becomes smooth again.
